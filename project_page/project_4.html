Project Details

Carrots and sticks: assessing intervention effectiveness for sustainable mobility systems to reduce GHG emissions

Objective

To combat climate change, governments worldwide are implementing a variety of policies to encourage sustainable transportation. These interventions range from "carrots" (incentives like EV subsidies and public transit grants) to "sticks" (disincentives like carbon taxes and congestion pricing). This project's objective is to systematically evaluate the real-world effectiveness of these different policy levers in reducing greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from the transportation sector.

Methodology

We employ a quasi-experimental research design, analyzing large-scale, longitudinal data from jurisdictions that have implemented different sustainable mobility policies. Using advanced econometric models, such as difference-in-differences and regression discontinuity, we isolate the causal impact of each policy on key outcomes, including vehicle-miles traveled (VMT), mode split, fleet composition, and overall GHG emissions. This allows us to compare the cost-effectiveness and behavioral responses associated with various incentive and disincentive schemes.

Expected Outcomes

The findings from this research will provide evidence-based guidance for policymakers on designing optimal policy packages to accelerate the transition to sustainable mobility. We will produce clear, actionable insights on which combinations of "carrots" and "sticks" are most effective at achieving climate targets while considering social equity and economic impacts. The results will be disseminated through policy briefs, academic publications, and a public-facing interactive tool for exploring policy scenarios.
