Transportation electrification in cold-climate regions

Objective

This project aims to develop a general, transferable framework for energy management and optimization in electrified public transit, with a focus on battery electric bus operations under real-world conditions. It will clarify how operational patterns, route context, and weather, especially cold-climate constraints, shape energy demand, reliability, and charging needs at the system level. By integrating insights on auxiliary heating and residual emissions sources such as fuel-fired heaters (FFH) where applicable, the project will support agencies in achieving net-zero transit goals while maintaining passenger comfort, service quality, and cost effectiveness. Xuelin Tian’s role is to lead the data-driven analysis and translate findings into decision-oriented strategies for transit electrification planning and operations.

Methodology

The study will synthesize multi-source datasets that describe transit operations and energy performance, then use data-driven modeling to characterize and explain variation in energy demand across routes, seasons, and service conditions. Rather than emphasizing a single modeling technique, the approach will prioritize interpretability and operational relevance, combining predictive analysis with driver attribution to identify which factors most strongly influence energy use and when those effects intensify under winter conditions. A complementary heating module will be incorporated to represent heating-related energy burdens and, where FFH exists, its fuel use and emissions contribution, enabling consistent assessment across technology and operational scenarios. The results will then be translated into optimization-oriented insights that connect energy forecasting to practical planning decisions, including charging coordination, duty-cycle matching, and winter operations strategies.

Outcomes and Impacts

The project will deliver a generalizable energy management framework that helps transit agencies anticipate and reduce energy variability in electrified bus operations, particularly under cold-weather constraints. It will produce evidence-based insights into key drivers of energy demand and the role of heating and energy recovery, enabling clearer guidance on where operational changes, control strategies, or technology choices can deliver the largest benefits. The outcomes will be packaged into decision-support-ready outputs, such as planning heuristics, prioritization guidance, and communication-ready summaries that can inform fleet deployment, charging strategy, and winter service planning. Overall, the work will strengthen the operational case for transit electrification by improving energy efficiency, reducing residual emissions, and supporting reliable, scalable zero-emission bus deployment.
