Towards Climate-Resilient and Healthy Cities: Integrating Mobility, Thermal Exposure, and Population Vulnerability

Objective

This project aims to develop a general and transferable framework for assessing and reducing pedestrian solar radiation exposure in urban walking networks. It will clarify how atmospheric radiation, building and tree shade, urban form, and street network structure jointly shape route exposure, travel efficiency, and the availability of routes with lower exposure. Building on preliminary work on climate responsive walking routes, the project advances toward a more physically interpretable representation of solar exposure and its variation across time and space. By linking route analysis with network analysis, the research will support cities in improving pedestrian comfort and climate resilience without requiring unreasonable travel detours.

Methodology

The study will synthesize multiple data sources describing pedestrian networks, hourly direct and diffuse solar radiation, building geometry, and tree canopy structure. Three dimensional shade modelling will be used to estimate the hourly sunlight conditions of sampling points distributed along the walking network, and these results will be aggregated to individual street segments. Solar radiation, shade availability, and segment length will then be combined to construct a time dependent exposure cost for each network edge. A spatially distributed set of origin and destination pairs will be analysed using multiobjective routing and Pareto filtering to compare shortest routes, routes with minimum exposure, and efficient alternatives between them. The route results will subsequently be aggregated across the network to identify recurring corridors with lower exposure, persistent exposure bottlenecks, and street segments with strong potential for shade interventions.

Outcomes and Impacts

The project will deliver a generalizable solar exposure assessment and routing framework that helps cities understand where pedestrian exposure can be reduced through route adjustment and where it is constrained by the existing urban environment. It will produce evidence based insights into the influence of time of day, building shade, tree canopy, and network structure on opportunities for exposure reduction. The outcomes will be presented through route tradeoff profiles, maps of corridors with lower exposure, exposure bottleneck indicators, and priority scores for street segments. These results can inform tree planting, shade infrastructure, and other pedestrian focused climate adaptation measures, while providing a practical basis for improving the comfort, resilience, and usability of urban walking networks.
